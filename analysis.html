Purpose of the Comparison

This page compares Norman‚Äôs Action Cycle with the GOMS model in order to understand how user goals, actions, and evaluations are represented in interaction analysis.

Conceptual Background

Norman‚Äôs model focuses on the user‚Äôs cognitive stages during interaction, while GOMS provides a structured description of tasks in terms of goals, operators, methods, and selection rules.

Purpose of the Parallel Analysis

The comparison table demonstrates how Norman‚Äôs Execution‚ÄìEvaluation Cycle and the GOMS framework align or diverge when applied to a practical example (toggling Wi‚ÄëFi). It shows how Norman‚Äôs cognitive stages map onto GOMS‚Äôs structured task elements, highlighting overlaps in goal setting, planning, and execution, while also revealing where Norman provides richer cognitive detail that GOMS abstracts away.

Alignment and Divergence

Three rows in the table are marked with a check (‚úÖ) because they represent strong conceptual matches:

  • Goal ‚Üî GOAL: Both models capture the user‚Äôs intention directly.
  • Plan ‚Üî Method & Selection Rules: Norman‚Äôs planning stage corresponds to GOMS‚Äôs decomposition into methods and choice logic.
  • Execute ‚Üî Operator: Both models acknowledge the physical action as a timed operator.

The other two rows (Specify, and Perceive/Interpret/Evaluate) diverge because GOMS does not explicitly model these stages. Norman accounts for them in detail, while GOMS assumes perfect perception and interpretation.

Role of Cognitive Detail

Norman‚Äôs model emphasizes the mental work users perform before and after execution: specifying actions, perceiving feedback, interpreting results, and evaluating outcomes. These stages are critical for understanding usability issues, especially the gulfs of execution and evaluation. GOMS, by contrast, focuses on expert routine performance and abstracts away these cognitive subtleties.

Implications for Usability Analysis

By blending Norman‚Äôs cognitive cycle with GOMS‚Äôs structured task analysis, designers gain a layered view: Norman explains why usability problems occur, GOMS structures how tasks are decomposed, and KLM predicts execution time. This integrated perspective helps identify mismatches between user goals and system actions, as well as gaps in feedback that hinder effective evaluation.
