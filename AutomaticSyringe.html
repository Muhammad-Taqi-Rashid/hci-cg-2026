Bad vs Good Syringe Keypad Design

This section compares two syringe dose entry designs — a numeric keypad versus increment/decrement buttons — using Norman’s stages of interaction. The purpose is to highlight how each design affects the gulf of execution and evaluation, and which HCI principles are applied or violated.

Gulf of Execution vs Gulf of Evaluation

This section explains the differences in usability between the two keypad designs in terms of Norman’s gulfs. The gulf of execution measures how easily users can carry out their intended actions, while the gulf of evaluation measures how easily they can interpret and verify the system’s feedback.

Bad Design (Numeric Keypad):
• Gulf of Execution: Small – easy to press keys quickly, but high risk of slips.
• Gulf of Evaluation: Large – weak feedback; users may not detect extra digits or mis-entry, leading to unsafe outcomes.

Good Design (Increment/Decrement Buttons):
• Gulf of Execution: Larger – requires more button presses, slower process, but actions are constrained and safer.
• Gulf of Evaluation: Narrow – feedback is incremental and localized; users can easily verify correctness of each digit, reducing risk of unnoticed errors.

Key Insights

The comparison shows that while numeric keypads allow fast entry, they increase the risk of slips and unnoticed errors — dangerous in safety-critical contexts like medical dosing. Increment/decrement buttons slow down execution but provide stronger feedback and error prevention, aligning better with HCI principles of visibility, safety, and user control.
